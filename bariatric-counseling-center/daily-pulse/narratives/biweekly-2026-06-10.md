# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-06-10

### The window in one sentence

Over 11 tracked days (May 17 -- June 9), sessions are modestly up 7.4% from the first half to the second half of the window, with two outsized traffic days (May 19 and June 3) doing most of the heavy lifting. The bigger story is the June 4 binge eating content cluster -- five pages published in a single day -- which is too new to judge but already showing early signs of engagement.

---

### Volume + composition

Total sessions across the window came to 251, with a per-day average of 22.8 sessions. Pageviews hit 950, averaging roughly 86 per day -- though that average is pulled upward by two significant spike days. Users totaled 217 (19.7/day). GSC impressions and clicks both read zero across the entire window, which is almost certainly a data pipeline issue rather than a genuine absence of search visibility. That zero should be investigated and confirmed before drawing any conclusions about organic search performance.

Channel mix for the window: Direct accounted for 130 sessions (52%), Organic Search for 88 (35%), Referral for 23 (9%), Organic Social for 8 (3%), and Unassigned for 4. Compared to BCC's baseline of roughly 60% Direct / 25% Organic / 10% Referral, Organic Search is running above baseline. That's a meaningful signal -- it suggests content published in earlier windows is earning search impressions and clicks even while GSC data shows zeros in the pipeline. Direct is running below the 60% baseline, which is worth monitoring. It could mean email and social promotion are lighter than usual, or it could mean a higher proportion of new discovery traffic is coming in, which would actually be a healthy sign.

Session duration anomalies are notable throughout the second half of the window. May 31, June 1, June 3, June 8, and June 9 all show average session durations running 85% to 207% above the 28-day median. That kind of sustained engagement depth -- even on lower-traffic days -- suggests people who are arriving are reading carefully, not bouncing. That's the right kind of visitor behavior for a clinical counseling center.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The window is gently accelerating. First-half sessions (May 17-21) totaled 121 across five days, averaging 24.2 per day. Second-half sessions (May 31 -- June 9) totaled 130 across six days, averaging 21.7 per day -- which looks lower on a per-day basis but is skewed by the two quieter June 8-9 close-out days. The 7.4% overall trend increase is modest and not cause for celebration, but it is directionally positive.

What makes the second half more interesting than the raw numbers suggest is June 3: 42 sessions, 230 pageviews, 38 users, and a session duration 132% above median. That's the kind of day that usually corresponds to an email send, a social post that landed, or a content piece getting shared. It occurred the day before the June 4 cluster publish, so it may reflect anticipation traffic or an untagged promotional push. The June 8-9 trailing days (17 and 14 sessions respectively) are soft but consistent with typical post-weekend patterns. The trend is not alarming. It's holding.

### Launches + efficacy

The headline content event of this window was a five-page binge eating cluster published on June 4. The pages are: "A Day Inside BCC's Binge Eating IOP" (`/self-improvement/mental-health/day-inside-bcc-binge-eating-iop/`), "Binge Eating Disorder and Ozempic: What Nobody Tells You" (`/self-improvement/mental-health/binge-eating-disorder-ozempic-what-nobody-tells-you/`), "Binge Eating vs. Emotional Eating: The Clinical Differences That Matter" (`/eating-disorders/binge-eating-vs-emotional-eating-clinical-differences/`), "Pre-Bariatric Surgery and Binge Eating Screening" (`/self-improvement/weight-loss-journey-self-improvement/pre-bariatric-surgery-binge-eating-screening/`), and "Binge Eating in Men: Why 40% of Cases Get Missed" (`/self-improvement/mental-health/binge-eating-in-men-why-40-percent-of-cases-missed/`). At six days old, none of these have GSC data available (likely pipeline-related), and GA4 sessions are not yet attributed at the individual page level in this data. These pages are too young to evaluate on search performance. Standard Google indexing lag for new content is 1-4 weeks. Check back at the 21-day mark.

The one content asset showing clear traction right now is the modified "Why Stress Eating Happens" page (`/self-improvement/mental-health/why-stress-eating-happens/`), also updated June 4. It has accumulated 26 GA4 sessions in 6 days -- the only launch in this window pulling measurable traffic. That's a meaningful early return for a page modification and suggests the update either refreshed its ranking position or triggered a re-crawl that improved visibility. No SERP position data is available to confirm, but the session volume is real.

The 30-day efficacy rollup shows 4 pages ranking in the top 3 and 4 in the top 10, with 181 GA4 sessions attributed to launched content. That's a thin but real foundation. Backlinks sit at 12 total inbound links across 4 referring domains, including 5 from .edu sources -- that's a strong ratio. Edu links carry meaningful authority weight, and 5 out of 12 total links being .edu is well above average for a local clinical practice. No new backlinks were recorded in this specific window, but the existing .edu link profile is an asset worth actively building on.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Audit indexing status on the June 4 cluster before next week ends.** Five pages published on the same day is a real production achievement, but if Google hasn't indexed them yet, they're generating zero search visibility. Submit each URL directly in GSC and confirm crawl status. The Ozempic/BED page in particular targets a high-intent query with strong current search interest -- it shouldn't sit in indexing limbo.

2. **Build internal links into the new binge eating cluster from existing high-traffic pages.** Fresh pages without internal links pointing to them are slower to rank and harder for Google to contextualize. Identify the 3-5 pages on the site with the most organic sessions and add contextually appropriate links into the June 4 cluster. The "Why Stress Eating Happens" page -- already pulling sessions -- is a natural hub to link outward from.

3. **Investigate the June 3 traffic spike source before treating it as organic baseline.** 42 sessions and 230 pageviews with a 132% above-median session duration is a genuine outlier. If this was an untagged email send or a social post, that content or format is worth repeating intentionally and tagging properly this time so it shows up in channel attribution rather than inflating Direct or staying unassigned.

4. **Pursue a second .edu backlink using the model that's already working.** With 5 edu links out of 12 total, BCC has demonstrated it can earn institutional-quality backlinks -- that's not common for local clinical practices. Identify which .edu source generated those links, understand what content or relationship created them, and replicate that outreach with one additional institution (UTSA, UT Health San Antonio, or a comparable San Antonio-area academic partner are worth a conversation).

5. **Treat the zero GSC impression data as a priority diagnostic item, not just a footnote.** If the pipeline issue is confirmed as a data gap rather than a genuine loss of search visibility, it needs to be resolved before the next rollup -- otherwise there's no way to evaluate whether the June 4 cluster is ranking, crawled, or invisible to search. That's the most important unknown in this window.
