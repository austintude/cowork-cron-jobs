### Volume + composition

The window produced 265 total sessions, 211 users, and 993 pageviews across 14 days. Per-day averages were 18.9 sessions and 15.1 users -- both below BCC's historical median of roughly 21 sessions/day. GSC data returned zero impressions and zero clicks for the entire window, which is almost certainly a data pipeline issue rather than a true signal; it should be investigated and ruled out before drawing any organic-search conclusions.

Channel composition is meaningfully different from BCC's expected baseline this period. Organic Search actually led with 122 sessions (46% of total), well above the expected ~25%. Direct came in at 92 sessions (35%), well below the expected ~60%. Referral was 33 sessions (12%, roughly on baseline), Organic Social was 16 (6%), and Unassigned accounted for 23 sessions (9%). The elevated Organic Search share is genuinely encouraging -- it likely reflects some of the recently published and submitted service pages beginning to surface in search results, even if GSC isn't reporting it. The suppressed Direct share warrants a look at whether regular internal users (staff, providers) had reduced activity, or whether a previously reliable email or direct-link touchpoint went quiet during this stretch.

The 23 Unassigned sessions (9%) are worth flagging. That volume is high enough to skew channel analysis. Untagged links from social posts, PDF links, or non-UTM emails are the most common culprits. Cleaning those up over the coming weeks would give a more accurate read on what's actually driving visits.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The window is declining. First half (June 14-20) produced 147 sessions at an average of 21/day. Second half (June 21-27) produced 118 sessions at an average of 16.9/day -- a drop of 19.7%. Pageviews tell the same story: 508 in the first half versus 485 in the second, a modest nominal drop that looks sharper once you account for the June 22 spike inflating the second-half number.

The pattern within the second half is particularly notable. June 21 was a critical low (5 sessions, -76% vs median), June 22 spiked hard (40 sessions, +90% vs median, 223 pageviews at +230%), and then the final four days of the window -- June 24 through 27 -- ran at 9 to 18 sessions daily with session duration collapsing to near-zero on June 27 (6 seconds average, -97% vs median). That June 27 engagement number specifically suggests either a bot sweep, a broken page, or a sharp session-quality drop that needs a same-day look in GA4. The overall second-half trend is concerning and worth watching closely in the coming week to see whether it stabilizes or continues.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Identify and shore up the one top-10 ranking page.** The 30-day rollup shows exactly one page in the top 10. Before producing new content, identify that page in GSC, review its current snippet and title tag, check its internal link profile, and make sure it's getting inbound links from related pages on the site. One well-connected top-10 page can pull significantly more sessions than it currently is with CTR optimization alone.

2. **Monitor the June 24 page modifications for indexing and early impressions.** Give these pages until roughly July 8-10 (two weeks post-modification) before evaluating their GSC trajectory. If they show zero impressions past that point, request re-indexing and audit for crawl or canonical issues.

3. **Investigate and resolve the GSC data gap.** Zero impressions across 14 days is almost certainly a reporting or connector issue, not an actual ranking collapse -- Organic Search was your top channel this window, which directly contradicts a zero-impression reading. Confirm the GSC property connection is healthy and that the date range is pulling correctly. This needs to be resolved before any organic-search conclusions can be drawn with confidence.

4. **Investigate the June 27 session duration crash (-97% vs median).** Six seconds average is not real browsing behavior. Pull the June 27 session breakdown in GA4 -- look for a single landing page generating a high volume of zero-engagement sessions, a redirect loop, or bot activity. If it persists into the coming week, it will distort all engagement metrics.

5. **Develop a plan to leverage the .edu backlink relationships.** Five .edu links is a strong starting point for a local behavioral health provider. Identify which institutions are linking and whether there are additional placement opportunities -- a second link from the same domain, a resource page listing, or a referral directory entry. .edu authority is difficult to build and worth protecting and expanding deliberately.
