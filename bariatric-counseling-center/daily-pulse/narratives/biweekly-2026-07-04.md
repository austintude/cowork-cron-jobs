# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-07-04

### The window in one sentence

Over the 14 days ending July 2, BCC averaged 16.7 sessions per day across 234 total sessions -- a modest, slightly declining volume overall, but the window closed with two strong days (June 29 and July 2) that suggest the second half may be stabilizing after a rough mid-window slump. GSC data is entirely absent for this window, which is the most pressing structural issue right now.

---

### Volume + composition

The 14-day window produced 234 sessions, 202 users, and 1,147 pageviews. Per-day averages of 16.7 sessions and 14.4 users are below what a site in active content growth should be seeing, but they are consistent with where BCC has been. The pageviews-per-session ratio works out to roughly 4.9, which is actually solid -- it suggests that people who do arrive are exploring multiple pages rather than bouncing immediately.

Channel mix for this window shifted noticeably from BCC's baseline. Organic Search led with 97 sessions (41%), Direct came in second at 88 (38%), Referral contributed 30 (13%), and Organic Social added 18 (8%). That's a meaningful inversion of the expected baseline -- Organic Search is typically expected around 25%, and here it outperformed Direct. That's a positive signal: it suggests content is picking up search visibility even without GSC data to confirm it. Direct coming in under 40% (versus the expected 60%) is also worth noting. It likely means fewer internal staff/practitioner visits inflating the numbers, or that email and direct outreach volume was lighter this window. Referral at 13% is above the 10% baseline, which is worth tracking -- 12 inbound links across 4 referring domains (including 5 edu links) are likely contributing there.

The 24 "Unassigned" sessions are a tagging gap. These are almost certainly from an untagged source -- a social post, an email send, or a QR code -- and should be investigated so they stop muddying channel data.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

Sessions declined 9.8% from the first half (123 sessions, June 19-25) to the second half (111 sessions, June 26 - July 2), so the headline trend is a mild decline. That said, the shape of the decline matters here. June 26-28 were genuinely bad days -- sessions dropped to 14, 9, and 4 respectively, with anomaly flags reaching CRITICAL severity against the 28-day median. Then June 29 reversed hard: 30 sessions, 339 pageviews, and average session duration of 305 seconds, all CRITICAL upside anomalies. The window then leveled into the 14-19 session range through July 2, which landed at 21 sessions and 98 pageviews -- also a CRITICAL upside flag.

The honest read: this is not a clean trend in either direction. There was a real valley mid-second-half that pulled the half-over-half number negative, but the window closed on a two-day run that looks more like a normal or above-normal traffic pattern. The June 29 spike in particular (339 pageviews, nearly 6x the daily median) needs a source explanation -- it is either a high-engagement content visit, a social share that drove a small burst, or an automated crawl. If it was real human traffic, the close of this window is more encouraging than the -9.8% headline suggests.

### Launches + efficacy

All activity captured in the launches ledger for this window consists entirely of automated daily/SEO/marketing pulse commits -- operational log entries, not content page launches. There are no net-new pages, no updated service pages, and no content assets that shipped during June 19 - July 2 with a URL that can be tracked in SERP or GA4. This is not a criticism of the commit cadence -- those operational commits have their purpose -- but it means there is nothing in this window to evaluate for content efficacy.

The 30-day rollup provides the more useful picture. Across the last 30 days: 12 pages launched, 61 pages modified, 77 git commits, 90,922 GSC impressions attributed to those launches, 251 GSC clicks, and 329 GA4 sessions attributed. One page is ranking in the top 3, two are in the top 10. Those numbers reflect real search traction developing from earlier launch activity. However, the complete absence of GSC data in the 14-day window (every single day reads 0 impressions, 0 clicks) makes it impossible to say which specific pages are currently ranking, whether positions improved or dropped, or whether impressions from those 12 pages are growing. This is a data pipeline problem, not a content performance problem -- but it blocks meaningful efficacy analysis until resolved.

Backlinks: 4 referring domains, 12 total inbound links, 5 of which are .edu links. Zero competitor links. Five .edu links for a bariatric counseling center in San Antonio is a meaningful signal -- those are high-authority sources. It is worth knowing specifically which .edu domains they are so you can assess whether there is an opportunity to deepen those relationships or replicate the outreach that earned them.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose and fix the GSC data gap first.** Every day in this 14-day window shows zero GSC impressions and zero clicks. This is not normal -- the 30-day rollup shows 90,922 attributed impressions, so the data exists somewhere. This is likely a property authentication issue, a date-range mismatch in the data pull, or a Search Console API connection that dropped. Until this is resolved, you are flying blind on organic search performance for the most recent two weeks. This is the highest-priority item for the coming week.

2. **Identify the source of the June 29 spike.** 339 pageviews on a single day -- roughly 5-6x the daily median -- either represents a meaningful traffic event (a share, a referral, a news mention) or a crawl/bot anomaly inflating the pageview count. If it was real traffic, knowing the source tells you something worth repeating. Check GA4's landing page report filtered to June 29 and cross-reference the referral paths.

3. **Run down the five .edu backlinks.** With only 4 referring domains total, each one is significant. The .edu links in particular suggest institutional credibility is building. Identify which institutions they are, understand what content they linked to, and determine whether a direct outreach to those contacts could yield a second link, a co-referral, or a resource page inclusion.

4. **Audit the "Unassigned" channel (24 sessions).** That volume is large enough to matter at BCC's current traffic scale -- it represents about 10% of all sessions. Adding UTM parameters to any email sends, social posts, or printed materials in the next campaign cycle will eliminate this blind spot and give you a cleaner picture of what is actually driving traffic.

5. **Plan the next substantive content launch with a specific target query.** The 30-day rollup shows 1 page in the top 3 and 2 in the top 10 -- real progress -- but the pipeline for this window contained no new content pages. With the GSC data issue resolved, the next strategic move is identifying which queries the top-10 pages are sitting at positions 6-10 for, then deciding whether a content update or internal link push could move them into the top 5 where CTR increases significantly.
