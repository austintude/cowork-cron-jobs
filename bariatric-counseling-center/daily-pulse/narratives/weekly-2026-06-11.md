Organic Search running higher than expected could indicate ranking improvement, but without GSC data to confirm, it is also possible GA4 is misattributing some sessions -- particularly if referral or social traffic is arriving without UTM parameters.

Referral at 18 sessions (12%) is worth watching. BCC's baseline is around 10%, so there is modest positive pressure there, likely connected to the 4 referring domains and 12 inbound links recorded in the backlinks totals. Five of those links are .edu domains, which is an above-average quality signal for a local counseling practice.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

On paper the window shows a 60% session increase from first half (58 sessions across May 21, May 31, June 1) to second half (93 sessions across June 2-3 and June 8-9). The framing is technically accurate but misleading without context: virtually all of the second-half volume is concentrated in June 3, which produced 42 sessions and 230 pageviews -- more than double any other day in the window. Session duration on June 3 was also 132% above the 28-day median, which suggests the people who arrived that day were engaged, not bouncing. The most likely explanation is an untagged email or social post that drove a burst of returning or warm-audience traffic.

Strip out June 3 and the window is flat to slightly soft. June 8 and June 9 both came in below the 28-day median for sessions and new users, and June 9 specifically flagged as CRITICAL for session duration (up 142%) alongside MAJOR drops in user count and new users. That combination -- fewer people, but the ones who showed up spent more time -- is a pattern consistent with a small, high-intent audience rather than broad reach. The overall trend is not accelerating; it is being propped up by one anomalous day.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Resolve the GSC data gap first.** Zero impressions across every day in a 7-day window is not a real traffic signal -- it is a broken connection. Until GSC is pulling correctly, there is no way to assess whether the content production cadence is producing ranking momentum or not. This is the prerequisite for everything else.

2. **Identify the June 3 traffic source.** 42 sessions and 230 pageviews with a 132% above-median session duration does not happen organically on a Tuesday without a trigger. Check for an email send, a social post, or a referral URL from around that date. If it was an untagged email, get UTM parameters on future sends -- that data is being lost every time.

3. **Map the 4 top-10 rankings to specific pages.** The 30-day rollup confirms 4 pages ranking in the top 10, but the briefing cannot name them without slug-level GSC data. Pull that report and identify which pages are holding those positions -- those are the pages that need internal linking support, strong CTAs, and no accidental de-indexing from future modifications.

4. **Give the GLP-1 and eating disorder cluster pages 2 more weeks before evaluating.** The June 9-10 modifications are too fresh to judge. Set a calendar reminder for June 24 to pull GSC impression and click data for `/glp-1-weight-regain-counseling-in-san-antonio-tx/`, `/tirzepatide-behavioral-support-in-san-antonio-tx/`, `/eating-disorder-treatment-in-san-antonio-tx/`, and the new night eating syndrome page. That is the first meaningful evaluation window.

5. **Explore a link-building outreach to one of the .edu referrers.** BCC already has 5 .edu inbound links, which is an asset most local practices do not have. Identifying which institutions those links come from and whether a second link or co-mention is possible -- a resource page addition, a guest contribution, a citation in student-facing mental health content -- is a lower-effort path to domain authority than cold outreach.
