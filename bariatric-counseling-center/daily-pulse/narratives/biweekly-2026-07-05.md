# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-07-05

### The window in one sentence

Over the 14 days ending July 3, the site averaged 15.9 sessions per day across 222 total sessions -- a fundamentally flat period shaped by two sharp one-day spikes (June 22 and June 29) rather than any sustained directional momentum. GSC data is completely absent for the entire window, which is the most pressing structural problem this briefing surfaces.

---

### Volume + composition

The window produced 222 sessions, 194 users, and 1,059 pageviews across 14 days -- averaging 15.9 sessions and 75.6 pageviews per day. GSC impressions and clicks both read zero across every day in the window. That is almost certainly a data pipeline or property connection issue, not a reflection of actual search visibility. Treating it as real would mean the site has had no organic search presence for two full weeks, which contradicts the channel data below. This needs to be diagnosed before the next rollup.

Channel composition this window: Organic Search led at 92 sessions (41%), followed by Direct at 82 (37%), Referral at 29 (13%), Organic Social at 18 (8%), Unassigned at 9, and Email at 1. This is a meaningful departure from BCC's expected baseline of ~60% Direct / ~25% Organic Search. Organic Search running at nearly double its expected share is potentially good news -- it suggests content published in prior weeks may be gaining search traction. But with GSC zeroed out, we cannot confirm which pages or queries are driving it. The lower-than-usual Direct share (37% vs. ~60% baseline) may simply reflect that the two big spike days (June 22 and June 29) pulled in more new users from search and referral, diluting the Direct proportion.

The Referral share at 13% is above baseline (~10%) and worth watching. With 4 referring domains and 12 inbound links total -- including 5 from .edu sources -- there is a real referral foundation here. If any of those .edu links are recent, they could be contributing meaningfully to both Referral sessions and eventual Organic Search authority.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

Sessions declined 10.3% from the first half (117 sessions, June 20--26) to the second half (105 sessions, June 27--July 3). On its face that reads as a mild pullback, but the story inside the halves matters more than the headline number. The first half was dominated by a single outlier day -- June 22 produced 40 sessions and 223 pageviews, roughly 2.5x the daily average. Strip that day out and the first half is softer than the second. The second half had its own spike on June 29 (30 sessions, 339 pageviews -- a 406% pageviews anomaly vs. the 28-day median) and a strong July 2 (21 sessions, 98 pageviews). The tail end of the window -- July 3 -- dropped sharply, though that is a Friday before a holiday weekend and consistent with expected seasonal behavior.

Pageviews tell a different story than sessions: 480 in the first half vs. 579 in the second, a 21% increase. That divergence -- sessions down slightly, pageviews up -- suggests that people arriving in the second half are browsing more pages per visit. Average session duration spiked on June 28 (+30% vs. median) and June 29 (+54% vs. median) before cratering on July 1 (-59%) and July 3 (-87%). The duration swings are volatile enough that no clean engagement trend can be drawn. The honest read: this window is neither accelerating nor declining in a meaningful way. It is volatile and spike-dependent, which is normal for a site at this traffic level but means individual content or referral events are driving the shape of the data, not sustained organic growth.

---

### Launches + efficacy

Every tracked launch in this window is a git commit -- daily pulse, SEO pulse, and marketing pulse entries logged by Daniel. There are no page launches, no content publishes, and no new URLs in the ledger for this 14-day window. The 30-day efficacy rollup tells a better story: 12 pages launched and 61 pages modified over 30 days, generating 96,278 GSC impressions and 268 clicks attributed across that broader period, with 1 ranking in the top 3 and 2 in the top 10. That is real traction, but it is concentrated in the period before this window's GSC data went dark.

Because the top_launches list contains only git commits with zero impressions and zero sessions, there is nothing in this window to evaluate for individual page performance. The efficacy system is not surfacing the content pages that are actually ranking -- the ones responsible for the 92 Organic Search sessions in this window. That is a gap in the data, not a gap in performance. The 30-day rollup's 96,278 impressions suggest meaningful search visibility exists; we just cannot see which specific pages are carrying it in this view.

The 5 .edu backlinks are worth flagging separately. Educational domain links are among the highest-authority signals a local health practice can earn. No new backlinks are attributed to this specific window, but those 5 links are doing long-term work that will compound. If any were earned in the past 30 days, that context would sharpen the Organic Search channel story considerably.

---

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC data gap first.** Zero impressions and zero clicks across 14 consecutive days is not plausible given 92 Organic Search sessions in the same window. Check whether the GSC property is still connected, whether the date range is off, or whether there is a pipeline issue in how GSC data is being pulled into the reporting system. Until this is resolved, the most important signal in the operation -- search ranking momentum -- is invisible.

2. **Surface which pages are driving Organic Search sessions.** Organic Search ran at 41% of traffic this window, nearly double BCC's baseline share. That is a meaningful shift that suggests prior content investments are paying off, but the efficacy data does not show which pages or queries are responsible. Pull a GA4 landing page report filtered to Organic Search for this window and identify the top 5 URLs. Those are the pages to prioritize for internal linking and content deepening.

3. **Investigate the June 22 and June 29 spikes.** Both days produced 2x-to-3x normal traffic with high pageviews-per-session ratios. Understanding the source -- a specific referral, a social share, an email send, a ranking jump -- is more strategically useful than anything else this window can offer. If either spike is repeatable, it represents a channel or content format worth doubling down on.

4. **Evaluate whether the pulse commit cadence is generating content the efficacy system can track.** The launches ledger is dominated by automated git commits with low confidence ratings. If substantive content pages are being published and not appearing in the ledger, the system cannot evaluate what is actually working. A manual audit of pages published in June against what appears in the efficacy rollup would clarify whether the 12 pages in the 30-day count are being tracked properly.

5. **Consider a structured internal linking pass on whatever pages are ranking in top 10.** The 30-day rollup shows 2 pages in the top 10 and 1 in the top 3. Pages ranking that well with only 268 attributed clicks across 30 days likely have CTR headroom -- either the title tags and meta descriptions need sharper hooks, or there is not enough internal link equity flowing to them to signal continued relevance to Google. Either way, those 2-3 pages deserve focused attention before any new content is commissioned.
