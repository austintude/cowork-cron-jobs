# Weekly briefing -- 2026-07-23

### The window in one sentence

The week of July 15-21 opened with three strong days -- sessions and pageviews running well above 28-day medians -- then collapsed sharply on July 18-19, leaving the window down 44% first half to second half. The pattern suggests a specific traffic event drove the early spike rather than a sustained trend, and the second half is the current baseline to watch.

---

### Volume + composition

Over the seven days (July 15-21), the site recorded 114 sessions, 108 users, and 385 pageviews. That works out to 16.3 sessions per day and 15.4 users per day. GSC impressions and clicks are both showing zero for the entire window -- this is likely a data pipeline or connection issue and should be verified before drawing any organic search conclusions.

Channel mix for the window: Direct led at 54 sessions (47%), Organic Search at 30 (26%), Referral at 19 (17%), Email at 6 (5%), Unassigned at 6 (5%), with AI Assistant and Organic Social each contributing 2. Direct is running below BCC's expected 60% baseline, while Referral is running above the ~10% baseline at 17%. That elevated Referral number is worth investigating -- with 4 referring domains and 12 inbound links total (including 5 .edu links), it's possible one or more of those .edu referrers sent a meaningful traffic pulse early in the week.

The near-complete absence of GSC data makes it impossible to assess organic search performance with confidence this week. Organic Search sessions (30) suggest Google is sending some traffic, but without impressions and ranking data to cross-reference, the quality and source of that traffic is unclear.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The halves split is stark. July 15-17 produced 73 sessions and 244 pageviews. July 18-21 produced 41 sessions and 141 pageviews -- a 44% decline in sessions across the second half. This is not a gradual slowdown; it's a cliff. Sessions on July 19 were 2 (versus a 28-day median of 20), and pageviews were 4 (versus a median of 53). That is near-total traffic absence for a Sunday, which could be partly day-of-week behavior, but the July 18 Saturday numbers (8 sessions, 28 pageviews) were also critically below median.

The first half's anomalously high session duration on July 15-16 -- 246 seconds and 491 seconds respectively against a median of roughly 110-112 -- suggests the early traffic was not casual or bot-driven. Someone (or multiple people) were reading deeply. Combined with elevated new-user counts on July 15-17, the early-week pattern looks like a referral or direct traffic event that brought genuinely engaged visitors. The second half returned to below-baseline, which means the window as a whole is not evidence of a trend -- it's evidence of an event followed by a return to a soft baseline.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Investigate the GSC data gap first.** Zero impressions across seven full days is almost certainly a tracking or authentication issue, not a real organic search collapse. Verify the GSC property connection in whatever pipeline feeds these reports before making any content or strategy decisions that depend on search visibility data.

2. **Identify what drove July 15-16 traffic.** Pull the Direct and Referral breakdowns in GA4 for those two days specifically. The session duration anomaly (491 seconds on July 16) and new-user surge suggest a specific source -- a newsletter, a social share, a partner referral -- sent engaged readers. If it was untagged email or social, that is a UTM tagging gap worth closing. If it was a referrer, that relationship is worth cultivating.

3. **Set a 14-day indexing checkpoint for both new posts.** The binge eating disorder page will hit 14 days around July 31. If GSC impressions are still at or near zero by then, submit for manual indexing and review the page for technical issues (canonical tags, noindex flags, internal link count). The ADHD page checkpoint falls around August 4.

4. **Build internal links into the binge eating disorder page now.** It is the most recently published piece in a high-intent cluster. Linking to it from existing eating-disorder category pages and any related posts increases the chance Google discovers and prioritizes it during the warmup period. This is a 30-minute task with an outsized indexing payoff.

5. **Treat the .edu backlink profile as an active asset.** Five .edu links for this site is above average for a regional specialty health practice. Identify which institutions those links come from and whether any have additional placement opportunities -- program pages, resource lists, faculty research citations. A second link from an existing .edu referrer is substantially easier to earn than a first link from a new domain.
