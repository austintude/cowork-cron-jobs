# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-05-20

### The window in one sentence

Over the three days May 17--19, sessions climbed sharply from 12 to 37 -- a 208% run-up across the window -- with May 19 landing 85% above the 28-day median and year-over-year gains confirming real growth, not noise. The underlying data infrastructure (daily pulse tooling, internal dashboards) shipped heavily this week, which is context for what comes next rather than a content win yet.

---

### Volume + composition

Over the May 17--19 window, the site recorded 73 sessions, 60 users, and 272 pageviews across three days, averaging 24.3 sessions and 20 users per day. GSC impressions and clicks show zero for all three days -- this is almost certainly a GSC data lag (Google Search Console typically trails GA4 by 2--3 days), not an indexing collapse. Do not read the zero impressions as a signal until data populates.

Channel composition for the window: Direct accounted for 43 sessions (59%), Organic Search for 25 (34%), Referral for 4 (5%), and Unassigned for 1 (1%). That broadly matches BCC's baseline of ~60% Direct / ~25% Organic, though Organic is running slightly above its expected share. That uptick is worth watching -- if it holds as GSC data fills in, it likely reflects content gains from the 11 pages launched in the last 30 days starting to accumulate search impressions.

Referral at 5% is thin but within normal range given only 4 total referring domains in the backlink profile. There are no unusual channel spikes that suggest untagged email or social traffic inflating the Direct number this window.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The halves data is empty in this payload, which is expected -- with only three days of data, a formal first-half-vs-second-half split is not meaningful. What the daily series does show is an accelerating ramp: 12 sessions on May 17, 24 on May 18, 37 on May 19. That is not a flat line or a random scatter -- it is a consistent upward step each day.

May 17 was a low outlier (sessions 40% below the 28-day median, new users nearly 43% below), while May 19 was a strong outlier in the opposite direction (sessions 85% above median, new users 100% above median, pageviews 91% above median). The May 17 dip is worth noting but not alarming -- low-traffic Sundays are common for a clinical services site. The May 19 surge is the more meaningful data point, and the year-over-year comparison (+60.9% sessions, +35.2% pageviews vs. the same day in 2025) confirms this is real forward progress, not just a bounce off a low baseline.

### Launches + efficacy

All launches captured in this window are git commits to the BCC daily pulse and dashboard tooling -- no new public-facing WordPress pages shipped May 17--19. The commits cover meaningful infrastructure: the internal operational dashboard, 7/14-day rollup views, history navigation, briefing tabs, valence-aware display, and the first LLM morning briefing. These are legitimate investments in how the team monitors and interprets the site, but they do not generate sessions or impressions on their own.

The 30-day efficacy rollup is where the real content picture sits: 11 pages launched, 8 rankings at top-3 position, 8 at top-10, and 49 GA4 sessions attributed to launched content. Eight top-3 rankings from 11 pages is a strong placement rate and the most quotable number in this window. Zero GSC impressions attributed in the rollup is a data-lag artifact consistent with the zero values in the daily GSC pull -- this number should repopulate as GSC catches up. No backlinks were earned inside this specific window based on the ledger, though the profile holds 4 referring domains, 12 inbound links, and notably 5 .edu links, which is a meaningful authority signal for a healthcare site.

The gap to watch: 49 attributed sessions from 11 pages over 30 days is light relative to 8 top-3 rankings. Pages ranking that high should be pulling more organic clicks. That mismatch suggests a CTR or snippet issue -- titles or meta descriptions that are not compelling enough in the SERP -- rather than a ranking or indexing problem.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Audit title tags and meta descriptions on the 8 top-3-ranking pages.** Eight top-3 positions generating only 49 sessions over 30 days is a CTR gap, not a ranking gap. Pull the GSC click-through rate per page once the lag clears, identify which pages are under-clicking relative to their position, and rewrite their SERP snippets to be more specific and action-oriented. This is the highest-leverage content task available right now.

2. **Establish a plan for growing the referring domain count beyond 4.** Five .edu links in a 4-domain profile is unusual and valuable -- it means at least one .edu domain is linking to BCC. Identify which institution that is, understand how that relationship formed, and consider whether a second outreach to that institution (or a similar one in San Antonio) is realistic. One targeted pitch is worth more than broad link-building activity.

3. **Set a GSC impression baseline check for May 21--22.** The zero-impression readings across all three days are almost certainly lag. Once data populates, compare actual impressions against the 30-day rollup to confirm the 11 launched pages are indexed and appearing in search. If any pages show zero impressions after the lag window clears, submit them individually via GSC URL inspection.

4. **Plan the next public-facing content push now that tooling is stable.** The week of May 17--20 was spent almost entirely on internal infrastructure (justified and necessary). With dashboards and daily briefings operational, the next sprint should return to WordPress content. The 30-day rollup shows the content model works -- 8 top-3 rankings from 11 pages -- so continuing that production pace is the right call.

5. **Investigate the session-duration volatility before drawing conclusions.** May 17 showed average session duration at 373 seconds (+225% above median), while May 18 dropped to 44 seconds (-61% below median). That swing is too large to be explained by content alone -- it likely reflects a small-number effect on a low-traffic day combined with possible bot or internal traffic on one of the days. Worth filtering out known internal IPs in GA4 if that has not been done, and checking whether the May 17 long-session traffic came from a specific page or source.
