# Weekly briefing -- 2026-07-13

### The window in one sentence

The week of July 6--12 delivered 164 sessions at a daily average of 23.4 -- solidly above the 28-day median on most days, driven by Organic Search and Direct traffic arriving in roughly equal proportions. The window ends on a soft note with July 12 collapsing to just 6 sessions, which is a known Sunday pattern rather than a structural signal, but session duration has been crashing site-wide since July 10 and that warrants attention.

### Volume + composition

Total volume for the window: 164 sessions, 148 users, 513 pageviews, and a pages-per-session ratio of roughly 3.1 -- which is healthy and suggests people are browsing, not bouncing. Daily average sessions of 23.4 cleared the 28-day median of 19 on five of seven days, so the week was genuinely above-baseline, not just carried by one spike. GSC impressions and clicks are reporting as zero across all seven days, which is almost certainly a data pipeline or property-connection issue rather than a real organic collapse -- given that Organic Search delivered 68 sessions this week, Google is clearly sending traffic.

Channel composition this week was unusual. Organic Search led at 41% (68 sessions), Direct followed at 40% (65 sessions), Referral contributed 8% (13 sessions), Email and Organic Social each added 8 sessions (about 5% each), and AI Assistant sent 2 sessions. The BCC baseline is roughly 60% Direct / 25% Organic Search -- this week those two channels essentially swapped positions. An Organic Search share this high suggests either a ranking improvement is in play or that some Direct traffic is being reclassified by GA4 (possible if a link from a cached or dark social share lacks UTM tags). The Referral number at 13 sessions is also worth watching -- it's running above the expected ~10% baseline and may connect to the 4 referring domains and 12 inbound links currently showing in the backlinks ledger.

The July 10 session-duration anomaly is the one genuinely concerning data point. Average session duration dropped from a healthy 203 seconds on July 7 to just 33 seconds on July 10 and has stayed depressed through July 12 (24--28 seconds). That's a 70-80% drop versus the 28-day median of 123 seconds. Sessions and users stayed elevated through those same days, so this isn't a traffic-quality problem in the traditional sense -- it looks more like a large share of visitors landing and immediately leaving without triggering meaningful engagement events. A staging-environment crawl, a bot flush, or a broken page experience could all produce this pattern. This needs a direct investigation before next week's content work.

The halves split evenly at 82 sessions each (July 6--8 vs July 9--12), producing a flat 0.0% session trend. That sounds neutral, but the pageview picture tells a more meaningful story: the first half generated 288 pageviews while the second half generated 225, a drop of roughly 22%. Combined with the session-duration crash starting July 10, the back half of this window is softer in engagement quality even though raw session counts held. This is steady-to-declining, not accelerating.

The honest read: the week opened strong (July 7 and July 8 both ran 60--90% above the 28-day median on users and sessions), the middle held reasonably well, and then engagement depth fell off. That's not a crisis -- it's a week that peaked early and then normalized. But if session duration stays this suppressed into next week, that's the signal to act on, not the session counts.

None of these are public-facing page launches in the traditional sense. ### What this means + what to do next

1. **Investigate the session-duration crash before anything else.** Duration dropped from 203 seconds (July 7) to under 35 seconds and stayed there through July 12. Before publishing new content or running any outreach, confirm whether this reflects a broken page experience, a bot or crawler surge, or a specific entry page that's loading improperly. Check GA4's pages-and-screens report filtered to July 10--12 to identify which landing pages are generating zero-engagement sessions.

2. **Push the near-me meta description from staging to production and request indexing.** The July 9 commit log shows this work was done on staging12 but there's no corresponding production-push commit in the window. With 195,205 impressions and a 0.4% CTR across the 30-day launch cohort, improving meta descriptions on ranking pages is the highest-leverage move available right now -- it doesn't require new content, just execution on work already done.

3. **Investigate why GSC is reporting zero impressions for all 7 days.** The site is clearly receiving organic traffic (68 sessions this week from Organic Search), so the GSC data pipeline is broken somewhere -- either the property connection, a filter, or a reporting delay. Fixing visibility into organic performance is a prerequisite for making good decisions about the content already live.

4. **Audit the 5 pages launched in the last 30 days for internal linking depth.** Two pages are ranking in the top 10 with strong impression volume but thin CTR. Beyond meta descriptions, check whether these pages are receiving internal links from higher-traffic pages on the site -- orphaned or weakly-linked pages often plateau in rankings because authority isn't flowing to them consistently.

5. **Build a plan to earn a second .edu backlink.** Five .edu links from 4 referring domains is the strongest part of BCC's current backlink profile. Identify which institution(s) those links come from, understand what content earned them, and replicate that outreach with one other local institution (UTSA, UT Health San Antonio, or a community college with a health sciences program). One additional .edu link in this domain category likely moves the needle more than five generic directory links.
