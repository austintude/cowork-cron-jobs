# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-07-01

### The window in one sentence

The June 16--29 window produced 253 sessions at a modest 18.1/day average, with the channel mix flipped from BCC's baseline -- Organic Search led at 43% of sessions rather than the expected 25%. The second half of the window declined sharply in volume before a single strong close on June 29, which signals an uneven but not broken traffic pattern rather than a systemic problem.

---

### Volume + composition

Over the 14 days ending June 29, the site logged 253 sessions, 210 users, and 1,219 pageviews. Per-day averages sit at 18.1 sessions and 15.0 users. GSC impressions and clicks are both zero for the entire window -- that is a data gap worth verifying (GSC often lags 2--4 days, and the 30-day rollup shows 74,725 attributed impressions, so this is almost certainly a reporting lag rather than a real zero).

The channel breakdown is the most interesting story here. Organic Search led with 110 sessions (43%), well above BCC's ~25% baseline. Direct came in second at 87 sessions (34%), below its expected 60%. Referral contributed 34 sessions (13%), a bit above baseline. Organic Social added 20 sessions (8%), and 24 sessions are sitting as Unassigned -- that last bucket is worth tagging, as it often represents untracked email or social traffic that could be attributed with proper UTM parameters.

The Organic Search lead is genuinely good news. It suggests the content work from the prior weeks is beginning to produce search-driven arrivals rather than just audience repeat visits. The lower-than-usual Direct share may simply reflect that the site attracted more new visitors this fortnight than usual, which is consistent with the June 29 anomaly showing 24 new users -- double the 28-day median.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

Sessions declined 26.7% from the first half (146 sessions, June 16--22) to the second half (107 sessions, June 23--29). On raw numbers that looks concerning, but context matters: pageviews were nearly flat across the halves (605 vs. 614), which means the people who did visit in the second half read more pages per session. The June 20--21 weekend dip (8 and 5 sessions) pulled the first-half average up somewhat, and June 22 was a 40-session outlier. Strip those and the halves are closer together.

The bigger concern is the June 25--28 stretch, where sessions ran 13, 14, 9, and 4 -- all flagged as CRITICAL or MAJOR downward anomalies against the 28-day median. Average session duration also collapsed on June 27 to 6 seconds, which suggests bot traffic or a crawl event rather than real visitors. June 29 then reversed hard: 30 sessions, 339 pageviews, session duration up 54% above median, and 24 new users. That single day was responsible for more than half of the second half's pageview total. The window ends accelerating, but the June 25--28 trough needs investigation before drawing conclusions about trend direction.

### Launches + efficacy

All confirmed launches in this window are git commits -- daily pulse reports, SEO pulses, and marketing pulse reports, all logged by Daniel Bisett. None carry public-facing URLs, so they do not produce direct session or impression data on their own. The notable exception is a June 25 commit titled "Records: mark 06-18 through 06-25 SEO work as pushed to prod + 6 new pages submitted to GSC," which confirms that at least 6 new public pages were pushed and submitted to Google during the window. Those pages are too new to show GSC data yet, and the zero-impression window totals are almost certainly the lag effect of pages submitted within the last week of the period.

The 30-day efficacy rollup gives the fuller picture: 12 pages launched, 61 pages modified, 74,725 GSC impressions attributed, 191 attributed clicks, and 310 attributed GA4 sessions across that wider window. One page is ranking in the top 3 and two are in the top 10. That is a thin but real foothold -- one strong-ranking page can drive outsized traffic once it settles into position. The 310 attributed GA4 sessions against 191 GSC clicks suggests some direct-load and social referral traffic is also touching those pages, which is a healthy sign.

What is not visible yet is which specific pages those top-3 and top-10 rankings belong to. That is the most important missing piece in this data set. Until you can tie a slug to a ranking position, it is hard to know whether to double down with internal links, update content, or leave it alone. On backlinks: the site currently has 4 referring domains, 12 inbound links, and 5 edu links with zero competitor links. Edu links are high-authority and hard to earn -- knowing which domains those are would directly inform outreach strategy.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Identify the 6 pages submitted to GSC on June 25 and build a tracking list.** These pages are now in their critical indexing window. If they are not showing impressions by July 7--10, request re-indexing individually through GSC's URL inspection tool. Tracking them as a named cluster will let you evaluate their performance as a group rather than as noise in aggregate numbers.

2. **Resolve which page holds the top-3 ranking in the 30-day rollup.** Pull the specific URL and query from GSC. That page is the most valuable asset on the site right now. Add internal links pointing to it from 3--5 related pages to protect and deepen the ranking before a competitor catches up.

3. **Investigate the June 25--28 session-duration collapse.** The June 27 average session duration of 6 seconds against a 198-second median is not a content problem -- it looks like a bot visit or crawl event that inflated session count without real engagement. Confirming this in GA4 (look for single-page zero-scroll sessions) will either clear the anomaly or reveal a real usability issue on whatever pages those sessions landed on.

4. **Chase the edu backlink sources.** Five edu links from 4 referring domains is a meaningful signal -- edu links typically come from resource pages, citations, or class syllabi. Identifying those domains and reaching out to the linking contacts about additional pages worth referencing is a lower-effort, higher-authority play than building new links from scratch.

5. **Audit the 24 Unassigned sessions and tag recurring untracked sources.** If those sessions have a referrer pattern (a specific newsletter platform, a social tool that strips UTMs, or a scheduling app), adding UTM parameters to those links will tighten your attribution and likely shift some of the artificially low Direct number back toward its correct source. Over time, cleaner attribution will make trend analysis more reliable.
