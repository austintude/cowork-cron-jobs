# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-06-11

### The window in one sentence

Over this 12-day window (May 17 -- June 10), sessions held essentially flat between the first and second half, but two standout days -- May 19 and June 3 -- drove most of the volume, and average session duration is climbing sharply in the second half, suggesting the people who are arriving are reading more carefully. GSC data is returning zeros across the entire window, which is a tracking issue that needs to be resolved before any organic search conclusions can be drawn.

---

### Volume + composition

The site recorded 271 sessions, 235 users, and 1,030 pageviews across 12 tracked days, for a daily average of roughly 22.6 sessions and 19.6 users. Those are modest absolute numbers, but the pages-per-session implied by the pageview total (about 3.8) is healthy and suggests visitors are moving through the site rather than bouncing on a single page.

Channel composition tilted more toward Organic Search than BCC's typical baseline. Direct accounted for 139 sessions (51%) -- below the expected ~60% -- while Organic Search drove 95 sessions (35%), meaningfully above the ~25% baseline. Referral contributed 26 sessions (10%), right at baseline. Organic Social added 8 sessions (3%) and Unassigned accounted for 5. The elevated Organic share is a positive signal and likely reflects early traction from the cluster of pages launched in the weeks leading up to this window. The below-baseline Direct share is worth watching -- it could mean an email or social push that normally drives Direct traffic did not go out during this period, or it could simply reflect normal variation.

The GSC zeros are a data problem, not a site performance problem. All daily impressions and clicks show zero for every day in the window. This is almost certainly a GSC property connection or reporting API issue rather than a sign that BCC dropped out of search results -- the Organic Search sessions in GA4 confirm the site is still receiving organic traffic. Resolving the GSC feed should be the first operational priority this week.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

Sessions were 137 in the first half and 134 in the second half -- a -2.2% change that is effectively flat. On a raw session count basis, this window is not accelerating. However, the pageview picture is more encouraging: first-half pageviews were 472, second-half were 558, a gain of about 18%. That means the people arriving in the second half of the window are viewing more pages per visit, which aligns with the anomaly data showing average session duration running 85% to 327% above the 28-day median on June 1, 3, 8, 9, and 10.

The honest read here is that volume is holding steady but engagement quality is improving. That is not a bad place to be -- it suggests the content that has recently gone live is resonating with the people who find it, even if it has not yet started pulling significantly more new visitors. If the GSC data were available, this would be the moment to check whether impressions are building even where sessions have not yet moved. Without that data, the engagement trend is the strongest positive signal available.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Resolve the GSC data feed before anything else.** Every strategic SEO decision for the next several weeks depends on impression and click data. Without it, you are navigating without a map. Confirm the Search Console property is connected correctly to the reporting pipeline and that the API credentials have not expired. This is not a content task -- it is a tools audit that blocks everything downstream.

2. **Begin internal linking the June 9-10 cluster to your highest-traffic existing pages.** The GLP-1, Mounjaro/Zepbound, Foundayo, ADHD/eating, and night eating syndrome pages are all topically connected to content BCC already has indexed and likely ranking. Adding deliberate internal links from older, established pages to these new pages will accelerate Googlebot discovery and pass authority before external links exist. Prioritize the bariatric surgery guide and the weight loss medications overview as link sources -- both are older pages with likely established crawl equity.

3. **Audit the 4 pages currently ranking in the top 3 and top 10 for click-through rate issues.** The 30-day rollup shows zero GSC clicks attributed, which -- even accounting for the GSC data problem -- suggests these rankings may not be converting impressions to clicks. Once GSC is restored, check whether the title tags and meta descriptions on those 4 pages are compelling enough for someone scanning search results to choose BCC over a competitor. This is likely a snippet/title optimization play, not a content rewrite.

4. **Plan a targeted outreach to add a second .edu backlink.** BCC already has 5 .edu links from what appears to be a single or small number of sources. The model is working -- an institution found BCC credible enough to link. Identify which .edu domain(s) those links come from and research whether UTSA, UT Health San Antonio, or another local institution has resource pages, wellness directories, or faculty research related to eating behavior, bariatric psychology, or GLP-1 counseling. A warm email from a clinician is more likely to land than a cold agency pitch.

5. **Schedule a content gap audit for the "telehealth in Texas" angle.** The `/telehealth-eating-disorder-treatment-in-texas/` page targets a statewide query, which is a meaningfully different search intent than BCC's San Antonio-specific pages. If BCC can serve clients across Texas via telehealth, this page has considerably more potential reach than a local page. Spend time next week researching what competing Texas telehealth practices are ranking for on statewide queries -- it may reveal whether this page is positioned to compete or whether it needs a different approach.
