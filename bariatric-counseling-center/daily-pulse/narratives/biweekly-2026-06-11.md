# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-06-11

### The window in one sentence

Over the 11 tracked days from May 17 to June 9, sessions are up a modest 7.4% (first half vs. second half), with two notable traffic spikes -- May 19 and June 3 -- accounting for a disproportionate share of that lift. The underlying pattern suggests periodic bursts rather than steady organic growth, and GSC data is entirely absent, which is the most pressing issue in the data right now.

---

### Volume + composition

Across the 11 days captured in this window, the site logged 251 sessions, 217 users, and 950 pageviews. That works out to roughly 22.8 sessions and 19.7 users per day -- numbers that are functional but not strong for a practice actively publishing new content. GSC impressions and clicks both show zero across every day in the window, which is not a normal data gap and needs to be addressed before anything else in this briefing can be fully interpreted.

Channel composition tilted toward Direct at 51.8% (130 sessions), with Organic Search contributing 35.1% (88 sessions), Referral at 9.2% (23 sessions), Organic Social at 3.2% (8 sessions), and Unassigned at 1.6% (4 sessions). Compared to BCC's baseline of roughly 60% Direct / 25% Organic / 10% Referral, Organic Search is running above baseline, which is a positive signal -- it suggests the content investment is beginning to generate search-driven visits. Direct is running below baseline, which could mean email and social promotion during this window was lighter than usual, or that some traffic that would normally be tagged as Direct is being captured under Organic. The Referral number is roughly in line with baseline.

The anomaly log flags 15 CRITICAL and 16 MAJOR events across the window, but most of those are not causes for alarm -- they reflect the two spike days (May 19 and June 3) pulling metrics sharply above median, and a corresponding softness on the days that follow. Session duration is the more interesting signal: it spiked dramatically on May 31, June 1, June 8, and June 9 (ranging from +85% to +207% above median) on days when session volume was average or below. That pattern -- fewer visitors staying much longer -- suggests engaged, intentional visitors rather than casual traffic, which is consistent with people researching behavioral support for GLP-1 use or eating disorder treatment.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The first half of the window (May 17-21) produced 121 sessions across 5 days, averaging 24.2 per day. The second half (May 31 through June 9, six days) produced 130 sessions, averaging 21.7 per day. The aggregate trend shows 7.4% growth in total sessions half-over-half, but the per-day average actually declined slightly. The growth is real but it is carried almost entirely by the June 3 spike (42 sessions, 230 pageviews -- more than double the median). Strip that day out and the second half is essentially flat or slightly down.

That is an honest read: the window is not accelerating in any durable way yet. The May 19 and June 3 spikes are encouraging because they confirm the site can generate high-engagement days, but the baseline between those spikes is hovering around 14-21 sessions per day. The late-window dip on June 8 (17 sessions) and June 9 (14 sessions) is worth watching -- those are below-median numbers accompanied by low new-user counts, suggesting the June 3 burst did not leave a lasting residue of returning or referred visitors. The trend is best described as "holding with periodic upswings" rather than compounding growth.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC data gap before the next content push.** Zero impressions across every tracked day for 11 days straight is not a data lag -- something is broken in how GSC is connected, how the property is verified, or how data is being pulled. Until this is resolved, there is no way to know whether recently modified and published pages are indexing, ranking, or generating impressions. This is the single highest-priority diagnostic item in the system right now.

2. **Request indexing for the June 9-10 batch through Google Search Console.** The eating disorder cluster pages, the night eating syndrome page, and the GLP-1 behavioral support pages are all strong candidates for manual indexing requests. Submitting URLs directly in GSC's URL Inspection tool shortens the typical 1-2 week crawl delay. This is especially worth doing for `/night-eating-syndrome-treatment-in-san-antonio-tx/` since it is a fresh publish with a specific local keyword target.

3. **Investigate the June 3 and May 19 spikes for source.** Both days produced well-above-median sessions and pageviews with no obvious channel explanation in the current data. Pulling session source/medium in GA4 for those two specific dates would reveal whether a referral, an email send, a social post, or a direct link share drove the traffic. If either spike traces back to a specific referral source or content piece, that is a replicable model worth deliberately repeating.

4. **Build a deliberate internal linking pass across the GLP-1 and eating disorder clusters.** The site now has multiple pages targeting Mounjaro, Zepbound, Tirzepatide, Ozempic/Wegovy, and Foundayo, plus eating disorder treatment and group therapy pages. These clusters will perform better in search when they link to each other with descriptive anchor text. Given the 4 pages currently ranking in the top 3, strengthening internal links between related pages could consolidate that authority and move adjacent pages up in position.

5. **Pitch a second backlink from an .edu or healthcare institution.** The site already has 5 .edu links, which is an above-average asset for a local practice. Identifying which institution(s) those links come from and finding a second contact or a related department to pitch -- with updated content on GLP-1 behavioral support or eating disorder treatment -- is a strategic play that compounds existing authority rather than starting from scratch.
