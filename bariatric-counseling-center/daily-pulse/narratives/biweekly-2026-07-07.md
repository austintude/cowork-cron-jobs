# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-07-07

### The window in one sentence

Over the 14 days ending July 5, BCC averaged 16.6 sessions per day across 232 total sessions -- volume that is essentially flat week over week (down just 1.7%) and running well below what the 30-day efficacy data suggests the site is capable of attracting. The dominant story is not decline but stagnation: Organic Search is now the leading channel at 45% of sessions, which is a structural shift worth watching, but GSC data is entirely absent for this window, making it impossible to confirm whether that organic lift is real or a tracking gap.

---

### Volume + composition

The 14-day window produced 232 sessions, 203 users, and 1,086 pageviews. Per-day averages sit at 16.6 sessions and 14.5 users -- modest numbers for a practice actively publishing content. Two days inflated the totals significantly: June 22 (40 sessions, 223 pageviews) and June 29 (30 sessions, 339 pageviews). Strip those two days out and the remaining 12 days averaged roughly 13.5 sessions and 44 pageviews per day. GSC impressions and clicks are zero across all 14 days, which is almost certainly a data pipeline or property-connection issue rather than a true reflection of search performance -- the 30-day efficacy rollup shows 116,961 attributed impressions and 370 clicks, so something broke in the GSC feed for this window.

Channel composition is the most interesting data point here. Organic Search delivered 104 sessions (45%), Direct delivered 80 (34%), Referral 29 (12.5%), and Organic Social 18 (7.8%). BCC's historical baseline is roughly 60% Direct / 25% Organic / 10% Referral. This window flipped that: Organic is now the top channel, Direct is running below baseline, and Referral is slightly elevated. The elevated Organic share suggests content published over the past several months is starting to accumulate ranking traction. The below-baseline Direct share likely reflects a quieter period for internal email or staff-driven traffic -- no email campaigns appear tagged in the data (Email shows just 1 session). The 9 Unassigned sessions are worth cleaning up; those are almost certainly under-tagged social or email touches.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

Sessions were virtually identical across both halves -- 117 in the first week (June 22-28) versus 115 in the second week (June 29-July 5), a -1.7% change that rounds to flat. This is not a trend; it is a plateau. Pageviews tell a slightly different story: the first half produced 498 pageviews and the second half produced 588, a roughly 18% increase in pages-per-session depth. That suggests visitors in the second week were browsing more content per visit, which is a mild positive signal for content relevance or internal linking -- but it is not strong enough to call momentum.

The second half was heavily shaped by two opposing days: June 29 was a genuine standout (30 sessions, 339 pageviews, average session duration up 54% vs. the 28-day median) followed immediately by a soft stretch through July 3-5, which included the July 4th holiday weekend. The holiday effect accounts for much of the apparent softness in the second half's daily shape. At the pattern level, the site is not accelerating -- it is holding steady at a volume that needs to grow.

---

### Launches + efficacy

Everything logged in the launches ledger for this window is internal tooling activity -- daily pulse commits, SEO pulse commits, and marketing pulse commits, all authored by Daniel Bisett and all carrying low confidence scores. There are no new public-facing pages, blog posts, or service pages that shipped during this specific 14-day window. That means this window cannot be evaluated for content-launch efficacy in the traditional sense; there is nothing new here to measure first-impression ranking or session attribution against.

The 30-day efficacy rollup does show real output: 12 pages launched and 61 pages modified across the broader 30-day period, with 116,961 GSC impressions and 370 clicks attributed. The rollup also shows 1 ranking in the top 3 positions and 2 in the top 10 -- thin for 12 pages, but not unusual at the 30-day mark when Google is still settling rankings. The more concerning number is the gap between impressions (116,961) and clicks (370), which works out to a 0.32% click-through rate. That is low. It suggests the pages are appearing in search results but not compelling clicks -- likely a title tag and meta description issue, not a rankings issue.

The backlink picture is stable: 4 referring domains, 12 inbound links total, and 5 edu links. No new backlinks appear to have been earned during this specific window, but the presence of 5 edu links is a meaningful signal -- edu domains are high-authority and suggest at least one institutional relationship is active. No competitor links were logged.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC feed before drawing any organic conclusions.** Fourteen straight days of zero impressions and zero clicks is a data problem, not a performance problem. The 30-day rollup confirms GSC is reporting data somewhere -- find where the connection broke and restore it. Until GSC is live again, you are flying without instruments on the organic channel that is now your top session source.

2. **Audit title tags and meta descriptions across the 30-day page launches.** A 0.32% CTR on 116,961 impressions is the clearest signal in this dataset. The pages are being seen; they are not being clicked. Rewriting titles to be more specific, benefit-forward, and human (consistent with BCC's people-first voice) is likely the highest-ROI hour you can spend this week.

3. **Investigate the June 22 and June 29 traffic spikes to identify the source.** Both days produced 2-3x the window average in sessions and pageviews, and June 29 also showed a 54% increase in session duration. If those came from a specific referral, social post, or email send, that source is worth deliberately repeating. If they came from organic, identify which page drove the sessions and prioritize strengthening that page's internal linking.

4. **Plan a content push targeting the top-10 ranking pages.** With 2 pages already in the top 10 and 1 in the top 3 at the 30-day mark, those pages are the most likely candidates for near-term session growth. Adding internal links from newer pages to those top performers, and ensuring their calls-to-action are clear and appropriately placed, could convert existing ranking momentum into measurable session and contact increases.

5. **Develop a strategy to grow the referring domain count beyond 4.** Twelve inbound links across 4 domains is a thin link profile for a practice competing in a metro market. The 5 edu links suggest an institutional relationship worth cultivating further -- consider whether there are other San Antonio-area health programs, community organizations, or patient advocacy groups that could be pitched for a resource mention or directory listing.
