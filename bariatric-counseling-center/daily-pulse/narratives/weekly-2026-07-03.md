# Weekly briefing -- 2026-07-03

### The window in one sentence

The week of June 25 -- July 1 generated 103 sessions across 7 days, with a dramatic mid-week dip followed by a sharp June 29 spike that pushed second-half volume 86% above the first half. The headline story is not momentum -- it is volatility, and the June 29 pageview anomaly (339 pageviews in a single day vs. a 67-pageview median) needs a source identified before it can be called a win.

---

### Volume + composition

Total volume for the window: 103 sessions, 92 users, 538 pageviews, averaging 14.7 sessions per day and 13.1 users per day. GSC impressions and clicks are reporting zero across all seven days, which is almost certainly a data pipeline or property connection issue rather than a true organic search blackout -- that needs to be confirmed and resolved before any SEO conclusions can be drawn from this window.

Channel composition shifted notably from BCC's baseline. Organic Search led the window with 48 sessions (roughly 47%), followed by Direct at 40 sessions (39%), and Referral at 10 sessions (10%). That Organic Search proportion is nearly double the expected 25% baseline, which suggests either a recent ranking improvement is pulling real traffic or that Direct is being suppressed -- possibly because some historically untagged traffic is now resolving differently. The channel split is worth watching next week to see if it holds.

Referral at 10 sessions is roughly in line with the ~10% expectation, and Organic Social at 4 sessions remains marginal. The 1 Email session confirms that email is not currently a meaningful traffic driver, which may be a gap worth addressing if the center has any list-based communication happening that is not being tracked or linked properly.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

On paper, the trend looks strong: second-half sessions (67) outpaced first-half sessions (36) by 86%, and second-half pageviews (445) dwarfed first-half pageviews (93) by nearly 5x. That would normally signal real acceleration. The problem is that both numbers are almost entirely explained by a single outlier day -- June 29, which logged 30 sessions and 339 pageviews against a backdrop where every other day in the window was at or below the 28-day median.

Strip out June 29 and the remaining six days average roughly 12 sessions per day, which is below the 28-day median of 20. The first half (June 25-27) averaged 12 sessions per day. The second half without June 29 averages about 12.3 sessions per day. That is not acceleration -- that is flat traffic with one unexplained spike in the middle. The honest read here is that the window is essentially steady at a below-median baseline, and the June 29 event is an anomaly that needs investigation rather than a trend to celebrate.

---

### Launches + efficacy

Every launch captured in this window -- and there are roughly 20 commits in the ledger -- is a pulse or SEO report document, all tagged as low-confidence git commits with no associated public URLs. None of them have recorded GSC impressions or GA4 sessions. That is expected for internal or tooling commits, but it means the launches ledger is not currently capturing the content that would actually move organic traffic. If substantive pages -- service pages, educational content, condition-specific resources -- are being published, they are not appearing in the tracker. That is the most important efficacy gap to address.

The 30-day rollup tells a more useful story: 12 pages launched over the past 30 days have generated 85,722 GSC impressions and 235 clicks, with 1 page ranking in the top 3 and 2 in the top 10. That is a meaningful signal that content production is generating organic visibility. The gap is that none of those specific pages are surfacing in this week's top launches -- likely because the tracker is sorting by recency and the pulse commits are crowding out the actual content pages. Cross-referencing the 30-day rollup pages against this window's GSC data (once the pipeline issue is fixed) would tell you which of those 12 pages drove the Organic Search volume this week.

Backlinks stand at 4 referring domains and 12 inbound links, including 5 .edu links. No new backlinks were recorded in this specific window, and no competitor links exist. The .edu link count is a genuine asset -- those carry real authority weight. Identifying which institutions hold those 5 links and whether any can be cultivated for additional mentions is a strategic play worth making.

---

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC data gap first.** Seven consecutive days of zero impressions and zero clicks is not a traffic story -- it is a tracking story. Confirm the GSC property is connected correctly in whatever pipeline feeds this briefing. Until that resolves, organic search conclusions for this window are unreliable.

2. **Identify the June 29 traffic source.** 339 pageviews on a single day against a 67-page median is a 5x spike. Check GA4 for which pages received traffic that day, which channel it came from, and whether a specific referrer, social post, or email send drove it. If it was organic, find the query. If it was referral, find the domain. This is the most actionable unknown in the window.

3. **Audit what the launches ledger is actually capturing.** The current tracker appears to be logging internal pulse and report commits as launches, which crowds out actual public content. Work with whoever manages the ledger logic to filter commit types so that substantive page publications surface clearly -- right now the 30-day rollup's 12 real pages are invisible in the weekly view.

4. **Map the 30-day rollup's top-performing pages to this week's Organic Search sessions.** You have 85,722 impressions and 235 clicks attributed to 12 pages in the 30-day window. Once GSC data is restored, match those pages to the 48 Organic Search sessions this week. That connection -- content launched, impressions earned, sessions arriving -- is the efficacy story worth telling Todd.

5. **Develop a .edu backlink outreach strategy.** Five .edu links from only 4 referring domains suggests concentration -- likely one or two institutions holding multiple links. Identify those partners, then research whether other San Antonio or Texas health-focused universities or programs could be approached. A second institutional referrer would meaningfully diversify the backlink profile and likely lift domain authority faster than additional content production alone.
