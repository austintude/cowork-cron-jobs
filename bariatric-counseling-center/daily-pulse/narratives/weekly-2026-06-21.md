### Launches + efficacy

The meaningful content work in this window happened on June 18, when five pages were updated: the weight-loss medications page, the holistic weight-loss/diet culture post, the cravings and impulse eating post, the self-esteem post, and the hydration post. All five are showing GSC impressions within three days of modification, which means Google is recrawling them promptly. That part is working.

The standout is `/healthy-living-2/why-hydration-is-essential-for-weight-loss-and-management/` -- 570 GSC impressions and 24 GA4 sessions in three days is a strong early signal. The ratio of sessions to impressions (roughly 4.2%) is meaningfully higher than the other four pages, suggesting the title and snippet are doing their job and drawing clicks. This page is worth monitoring weekly; if it sustains, it becomes a model for how to structure titles on the other posts.

The weight-loss medications page (`/understanding-weight-loss-medications-wegovy-ozempic-zepbound-and-mounjaro/`) is the most urgent concern. 1,112 GSC impressions with zero clicks is a significant mismatch. The page is appearing in search results -- Google thinks it's relevant -- but searchers are passing it over. That almost always means the title tag or meta description isn't matching what the person expects to find, or a competitor's snippet looks more useful. The self-esteem page (660 impressions, 3 clicks, 0.5% CTR) and the holistic weight-loss post (250 impressions, 1 click, 0.4% CTR) have similar CTR problems, though at lower impression volumes. These are not indexing problems -- they are snippet and positioning problems.

The 30-day efficacy rollup shows 26 pages launched, 65 modifications, and zero pages ranking in the top 10. That's expected at this stage of a content build -- Google's trust cycle for a newer or recently refreshed page typically runs 4--12 weeks -- but it means the next month is critical for seeing whether the current investment converts into rankings.

No new backlinks were added within this specific window. The existing profile of 4 referring domains, 12 inbound links, and 5 .edu links is stable. The .edu link count is a genuine asset for a health-adjacent practice; that's above average for a local counseling center.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Rewrite the title tag and meta description on the weight-loss medications page.** 1,112 impressions with zero clicks is a clear signal the snippet isn't compelling. Look at what position the page is appearing at in GSC, then examine the top 3 results for the queries it's ranking for. The rewrite should directly address what someone searching those terms is trying to decide -- not describe what the page contains.

2. **Use the hydration page as a structural template for the other four June 18 updates.** Pull the title format, opening paragraph structure, and internal linking pattern from that page and apply it to the cravings, self-esteem, and holistic weight-loss posts. Those three pages are getting impressions but not clicks -- the hydration page is proof the formula can work on this site.

3. **Investigate the June 11 spike before attributing it to content.** 178 pageviews and 261-second average session duration on a single day, with no obvious content launch that day, suggests an untagged traffic source -- possibly an email send, a social share, or a referral that isn't resolving cleanly. Identifying the source matters because if it was an email, you have an audience that's engaging deeply and you're not measuring it.

4. **Build internal links from the hydration and medications pages into the deeper self-improvement and mental health content cluster.** The second-half session acceleration suggests organic traffic is starting to move through the site, but the 30-day rollup shows zero top-10 rankings. Internal linking from higher-traffic pages into the cluster helps Google understand content relationships and can lift rankings on pages that are currently sitting at position 11--20 without clicks.

5. **Establish a backlink outreach target for the next 30 days.** Four referring domains is a thin profile for a practice competing on health-related queries. Given the existing .edu links, there's likely a pathway to one or two more institutional or community health links -- think local hospital systems, university health programs, or regional mental health coalitions. One well-placed .edu or .org link from a San Antonio-area institution would have outsized impact on domain authority relative to the current baseline.
