If that holds when GSC data is restored, it would suggest search traffic is quietly growing relative to the site's overall mix. ### Trend (first half vs second half)

The halves split cleanly: 137 sessions (May 17-21 + May 31) vs. 134 sessions (June 1-10), a -2.2% change. On pure session volume, this window is flat -- no acceleration, no meaningful decline. However, pageviews tell a slightly different story: 472 in the first half vs. 558 in the second half, a 18% increase. That divergence -- same number of people, more pages viewed -- is reflected in the session duration anomalies clustering in the second half. On June 8, 9, and 10, average session duration ran 134%, 143%, and 327% above the 28-day median respectively. People who did show up in the second half of this window were reading more and staying longer.

That combination -- flat sessions, rising pageviews, rising engagement depth -- suggests the site is not yet pulling in more new visitors but is doing a better job holding the ones who arrive. That is a reasonable position for a content-maturing site. The concern is that new user counts on June 8 (-40%) and June 9 (-36%, -35% new users) were notably below median, meaning the site leaned heavily on returning visitors in the back half of the window. Sustained growth requires both.

If it was organic, that is a ranking signal. If it was Direct or Social, there may be an audience to re-engage.

Identifying which institution(s) those links come from -- and whether there is a logical second link opportunity (a resource page, a student mental health directory, a clinical training partnership) -- is a strategic play that compounds over time. One well-placed .edu link in the binge eating or bariatric space would directly support the cluster that just launched.
