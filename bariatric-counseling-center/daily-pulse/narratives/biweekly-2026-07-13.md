# Biweekly briefing -- 2026-07-13

### The window in one sentence

Over the 14 days ending July 12, sessions accelerated 42.6% from the first half to the second (115 vs. 164), driven by a sustained four-day spike in new users that suggests a meaningful audience discovery moment -- but session duration collapsed in the final three days, which raises a quality question worth investigating. ### Volume + composition

The window produced 279 total sessions, 255 users, and 1,101 pageviews at a daily average of 19.9 sessions and 18.2 users. Those headline numbers are modest in absolute terms but the internal shape of the window matters: the second half outperformed the first by a wide margin, and the July 7-10 cluster in particular (32, 31, 24, 32 sessions on consecutive days) was well above the 28-day median of roughly 19 sessions per day. Pageviews averaged 78.6 per day, though the June 29 outlier (339 pageviews in a single day) is carrying a lot of that weight -- strip it out and the average drops to roughly 58, which is closer to baseline.

Channel composition shifted noticeably from BCC's expected baseline. Organic Search led the window at 123 sessions (44% of total), well above the expected 25% share, while Direct came in at 116 sessions (42%), below its usual 60% dominance. Referral accounted for 17 sessions (6%), Organic Social 13 (5%), Email 8 (3%), and AI Assistant 2 sessions. The elevated Organic Search share is the most significant pattern here -- it suggests ranking momentum rather than a one-day spike. The lower-than-usual Direct share may simply reflect the holiday week in the first half (July 3-4 logged just 8 and 9 sessions respectively, likely suppressing the brand-aware repeat-visitor traffic that typically inflates Direct).

No GSC impressions or clicks are registering for any day in the window. This is not a ranking collapse -- it is almost certainly a data pipeline or property authentication issue. Until GSC data flows again, organic search performance cannot be evaluated with any confidence.

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The window is accelerating. The first half (June 29 through July 5) totaled 115 sessions, pulled down by the July 3-4 Independence Day dip (8 and 9 sessions) that is expected and not concerning. Excluding those two holiday days, the first half averaged about 21 sessions per day. The second half (July 6 through July 12) totaled 164 sessions, and the July 7-10 stretch registered four consecutive days at or above 24 sessions -- each flagged as CRITICAL or MAJOR anomalies against the 28-day median. New users spiked 92-125% above median on July 7, 8, and 10, which is the clearest indicator of audience expansion: these are not repeat visitors cycling through.

The concern is what happened at the tail. July 11 and 12 show session duration collapsing -- 24 seconds and 28 seconds per session respectively, against a 28-day median of roughly 123 seconds. That 73-80% drop in engagement depth coincides with the high new-user counts, which suggests the traffic arriving in those final days may be lower-intent or landing on pages that are not meeting expectations. The acceleration is real and positive. The quality drop at the end of the window is a flag worth watching, not a reason to sound an alarm yet.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC data gap before anything else.** Fourteen consecutive days of zero impressions and zero clicks is not a traffic problem -- it is a reporting problem. Verify the GSC property is still authenticated in GA4's Search Console linkage, confirm the property has not been re-verified or ownership changed, and check whether the data is simply delayed. Until this is resolved, you are making organic search decisions blind.

2. **Investigate the session-duration collapse on July 10-12.** High new-user counts paired with sub-30-second session durations suggests a traffic source that is not converting to engagement -- possibly a social post, an AI-generated referral, or a backlink from a low-context source landing people on a page that does not match their intent. Pull the landing page report for July 10-12 specifically and identify which pages received the bulk of those short sessions.

3. **Push the July 9 staging work to production and document it as a trackable launch.** The internal-linking additions and near-me meta description update are exactly the kind of on-page changes that can shift click-through rate on pages already ranking in positions 4-10. Right now it is sitting in staging with no measurable impact. Get it live, note the exact date, and plan to evaluate GSC CTR for those pages in 3-4 weeks.

4. **Map the 5 EDU backlinks.** Five EDU links is an unusually strong signal for a local practice. Identify which domains they are, which BCC pages they point to, and whether there are relationship pathways (faculty contacts, student health partnerships, academic program collaborations) that could yield additional links or content co-creation opportunities. This is a slow-burn play but EDU authority compounds.

5. **Develop a hypothesis about what drove July 7-10.** Four consecutive days of new-user volume at 60-125% above median does not happen without a cause. Candidates: a LinkedIn post gaining traction (the July 13 commit logs a LinkedIn ideas send for that week, suggesting the content calendar was active), a referral from one of the 4 known referring domains, or a Reddit or forum mention. Identifying the source tells you what to repeat -- or what to build a more intentional version of.
