# Weekly briefing -- 2026-07-04

### The window in one sentence

The week of June 26 -- July 2 was defined by a volatile first half (sessions bottomed out at 4 on June 28) followed by a strong recovery that ended the window on an upward slope. The second half outpaced the first by 211%, though absolute volume remains well below the 28-day median, and GSC data shows zero impressions for the entire window -- a data gap that needs to be resolved before organic performance can be assessed.

---

### Volume + composition

Total sessions for the 7-day window came in at 111, with 101 users and 607 pageviews. On a per-day basis that averages 15.9 sessions and 14.4 users -- both running below the 28-day median of roughly 20 sessions/day. The pageview-to-session ratio is notable: 5.5 pageviews per session, which is higher than typical, driven almost entirely by June 29's outsized day (339 pageviews across 30 sessions, an average of 11.3 pages per session). That single day accounts for 56% of the window's total pageviews.

Channel composition landed at 45% Organic Search, 45% Direct, 5% Referral, and 5% Organic Social. That's a significant departure from BCC's expected baseline of ~60% Direct / ~25% Organic Search. Organic Search punching up to near-parity with Direct suggests either a quiet ranking improvement or that Direct traffic has softened -- possibly due to reduced email or social sends during this period. The Referral share (6 sessions) is below baseline but not alarming. Organic Social at 5 sessions is consistent with low-volume expectations.

GSC impressions and clicks show zero across all seven days. This is almost certainly a data pipeline issue rather than a real-world collapse in search visibility -- the 30-day efficacy rollup credits 90,922 impressions to attributed launches, which would be impossible if the site had genuinely dropped out of search. Flag this with whoever manages the GSC integration before drawing any conclusions about organic search health.

---

### Trend (first half vs second half)

The halves split tells an important story. The first half (June 26-28) totaled just 27 sessions and 86 pageviews -- a three-day stretch that generated multiple CRITICAL anomalies and hit as low as 4 sessions on June 28. The second half (June 29 -- July 2) totaled 84 sessions and 521 pageviews, a 211% jump in session volume. That's genuine acceleration, not noise.

What makes this encouraging rather than just a June 29 blip is that July 1 (19 sessions) and July 2 (21 sessions, 98 pageviews) both held above the daily median, suggesting the recovery has some legs. The YoY picture is more complicated -- several days are still down 35-67% compared to the same days last year -- but the intra-window trajectory is pointed in the right direction. The week ended stronger than it started, and that's the pattern worth watching.

### Launches + efficacy

All launches captured in this window are git commits -- daily pulse, SEO pulse, and marketing pulse entries authored by Daniel Bisett. These are internal tracking and tooling commits, not public-facing content pages. None carry a public URL, none have GSC impressions, and none have GA4 sessions attributed. That's expected for commits of this type; they're infrastructure work, not content launches.

The more meaningful signal sits in the 30-day efficacy rollup. Across the last 30 days, 12 pages were launched and 61 pages were modified, producing 90,922 GSC impressions and 251 clicks attributed. One ranking sits in the top 3 positions and two sit in the top 10. That's a thin but real organic footprint -- most of the 12 launched pages are likely still in Google's indexing and ranking queue, which typically runs 4-8 weeks for a site of this authority. The fact that 329 GA4 sessions are attributed to launched content in the 30-day window means some pages are already converting visits, even if rankings haven't fully settled.

No new backlinks were earned within this 7-day window. The current backlink profile stands at 4 referring domains, 12 inbound links, and 5 .edu links. The .edu count is disproportionately strong for a site this size and should be treated as a meaningful authority signal. No competitor links are present.

### What this means + what to do next

1. **Diagnose the GSC data gap before anything else.** Seven straight days of zero impressions in the pipeline is a reporting failure, not a traffic failure -- the 30-day rollup confirms the site is visible in search. Identify whether the GSC API connection dropped, a property verification lapsed, or a date-range offset crept into the query. Until this is resolved, organic trend analysis is flying blind.

2. **Investigate June 29 specifically.** Thirty sessions with an average of 11.3 pageviews per session and a 54% above-median session duration is unusual behavior. This was likely a single engaged user or a small group moving through the site deliberately -- possibly a referral source, a sales prospect, or an internal review. Pull the session-level data in GA4 to identify the source and what pages were visited. If it was a genuine external referral, that referrer deserves follow-up.

3. **Prioritize internal linking across the pages launched in the last 30 days.** With 12 new pages live and two already in the top 10, the cluster likely needs stronger internal linking to help Google understand topical relationships and to move link equity toward pages sitting just outside top-10 positions. This is a high-leverage, low-cost move at this stage of the content build.

4. **Pursue a second .edu backlink.** BCC already carries 5 .edu inbound links -- more than most comparable practices. That means there's a demonstrated willingness in the academic / research community to link here. Identify which institution or program produced the existing .edu links and look for a second contact at the same institution, or a peer institution in San Antonio (UTSA, UT Health, Trinity) where a resource page, counseling department, or student health center might link to relevant BCC content.

5. **Assess whether the first-half session drop (June 26-28) correlates with a specific channel going quiet.** If Direct traffic softened because an email list wasn't mailed or a social post wasn't scheduled, that's a fixable pattern -- not an organic problem. A quick channel-level breakdown of those three days against the second half would confirm whether the recovery was organic-driven or Direct-driven, and that answer shapes content priorities for the coming month.
